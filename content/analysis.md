# Mapping the AI Regulation Landscape: A Comparative Analysis of Major U.S. Proposals (2024–2026)

*Last updated: March 20, 2026*

---

## Introduction

The United States is in the middle of a defining policy moment for artificial intelligence. Between early 2024 and early 2026, a remarkable number of AI governance proposals have emerged from the White House, the U.S. Senate, the U.S. House, and state legislatures — each reflecting different theories about what the core problem is, who should solve it, and how urgently it needs solving.

This analysis maps the current landscape of major AI regulation proposals across three analytical dimensions: governance structure (who regulates and how), scope and application (what gets regulated and when), and political positioning (the underlying theory of the problem). We examine nine proposals in detail, identify the axes of agreement and disagreement, and place them on comparative 2×2 frameworks to reveal the emerging contours of the debate.

The proposals surveyed are:

1. **White House AI Legislative Framework** (March 2026)
2. **Blackburn TRUMP AMERICA AI Act** (2026)
3. **OpenAI / Chris Lehane Policy Position** (March 2026)
4. **Sen. Mark Kelly — "AI for America" Roadmap** (September 2025)
5. **Sen. Bernie Sanders — AI Policy Proposals** (2025–2026)
6. **Rep. Ro Khanna — Seven Principles for Democratic AI** (February 2026)
7. **California SB 1047** (2024, vetoed) and **SB 53** (2025, signed)
8. **New York RAISE Act** (2025, signed)

## Part I: Summary of Each Proposal

### 1. White House AI Legislative Framework (March 2026)

The White House released legislative recommendations outlining what it considers a "National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence." The document is structured around seven pillars, each addressed to Congress.

**Key provisions:**

- **Child safety**: Commercially reasonable age-assurance requirements; platforms must reduce risks of exploitation and self-harm to minors; affirm existing child privacy protections apply to AI
- **Community protection**: Residential ratepayers must not bear increased electricity costs from data centers; streamlined federal permitting for AI infrastructure; combat AI-enabled fraud targeting seniors
- **Intellectual property**: The Administration believes AI training on copyrighted material does not violate copyright but supports letting courts resolve the question; supports collective licensing frameworks without requiring them; proposes a federal digital replica protection framework
- **Anti-censorship**: Prevent government coercion of AI providers to alter content; provide redress for agency censorship efforts
- **Innovation**: Establish regulatory sandboxes; make federal datasets AI-ready; fund testing environments and compute resources for startups
- **Workforce**: Non-regulatory methods to incorporate AI into education and training programs; expand labor statistics on AI-driven workforce changes
- **Federal preemption**: Preempt state AI laws that impose "undue burdens"; states may not regulate AI development (deemed inherently interstate); states retain police powers for general applicability laws (fraud, child protection, consumer protection), zoning, and their own procurement

**Regulatory philosophy**: Light-touch, innovation-first. The framework explicitly avoids creating a new regulatory body or mandatory compliance regime for AI developers. It positions state regulation as the primary threat to U.S. competitiveness and frames preemption as the central legislative priority. Enforcement of existing laws, not new AI-specific mandates, is the preferred mechanism.

---

### 2. Blackburn TRUMP AMERICA AI Act (2026)

Sen. Marsha Blackburn introduced the most comprehensive federal AI bill to date, spanning 17 titles and hundreds of pages. Despite being framed as implementing the White House's deregulatory vision, the bill contains significantly more regulatory density than the White House framework suggests.

**Key provisions:**

- **Duty of care** (Title I): AI chatbot developers must exercise "reasonable care" in design and operation to prevent reasonably foreseeable harms; FTC promulgates minimum safeguards; enforcement by FTC and state attorneys general
- **Job effects reporting** (Title II): Publicly-traded companies and large private companies must disclose quarterly AI-related workforce changes (layoffs, hires, reskilling, unfilled positions) to the Bureau of Labor Statistics
- **Section 230 sunset** (Title III): Full repeal of Section 230 of the Communications Act
- **Child safety** (Titles IV–V): Incorporates the Kids Online Safety Act; prohibits minor use of AI companions; establishes Kids Online Safety Council
- **Risk-based framework** (Title VI): Advanced AI evaluation program; obligation to participate in evaluations with enforcement and penalties
- **Liability** (Title VII): Developer liability for harm to business or consumer; deployer liability; federal cause of action; foreign AI developer registration requirement
- **Bias mitigation** (Title VIII): Audits for bias and training on ethics
- **Innovation support** (Title IX): NIST Center for AI Standards and Innovation; international cooperation; regulatory barrier identification; research funding
- **National AI Research Resource** (Title X): Establishes a national research resource
- **Ratepayer protection** (Title XI): Consumer protections for data center infrastructure costs
- **Digital replicas** (Title XII): NO FAKES Act — voice and visual likeness rights
- **Copyright** (Titles XIV–XV): Content provenance requirements; clarification on AI training and fair use; unauthorized AI-generated derivative works deemed infringing
- **Federal preemption** (Title XVII): Preempts state laws governing frontier AI risk management and digital replicas; does not preempt general applicability laws

**Regulatory philosophy**: Comprehensive and interventionist, despite the deregulatory framing. The bill creates multiple enforcement pathways (FTC, state AGs, private right of action), mandatory reporting obligations, and a risk-based evaluation program. The tension between its stated purpose (eliminating regulatory chaos) and its actual scope (creating substantial new federal obligations) is one of its most notable features.

### 3. OpenAI / Chris Lehane Policy Position (March 2026)

OpenAI's position, articulated through its "The Prompt" newsletter by Chief Global Affairs Officer Chris Lehane and Head of National Security Policy Sasha Baker, advocates for a specific sequencing of governance approaches.

**Key positions:**

- **Federal framework first**: Legislation requiring frontier labs to test systems using classified government capabilities; national safety standards addressing catastrophic risks and child protection; targeted preemption to support national standards
- **State alignment second**: In the absence of federal action, states should align around the emerging California/New York model rather than diverge
- **Federal incentive third**: State laws should include mechanisms recognizing that federal testing satisfies state requirements, creating incentives for a national framework
- **CAISI as cornerstone**: The Center for AI Standards and Innovation should serve as the primary evaluative institution, testing and evaluating advanced systems before deployment
- **Prevention over liability**: The federal government can move from a liability model (punish after harm) to a prevention model (test before deployment) because it has technical expertise, threat intelligence, and access to classified systems that states lack
- **Global institutional network**: AI Safety Institutes worldwide should form a coordinated network for sharing evaluation methods and enabling continuous independent analysis

**Regulatory philosophy**: Prevention-first with strong federal institutional capacity. OpenAI's position is notable for endorsing a meaningful federal testing regime — not merely voluntary commitments — while arguing that only the federal government has the capabilities to implement effective pre-deployment evaluation. This positions them to the left of the White House framework on federal oversight while supporting preemption of state regulation.

### 4. Sen. Mark Kelly — "AI for America" Roadmap (September 2025)

Kelly's roadmap is the most developed Democratic proposal for AI governance, focusing heavily on worker protection and economic redistribution alongside safety and competitiveness.

**Key proposals:**

- **AI Horizon Fund**: A federal fund financed by contributions from leading AI companies, used to reinvest in workers, infrastructure, and responsible deployment. Revenue generated from industry profits funds worker retraining through union apprenticeships, community college programs, and public-private partnerships
- **Worker transition support**: Advance notice requirements for AI-driven workforce changes; strengthened safety nets including enhanced unemployment aid; BLS data collection expansion on retraining outcomes, deployment patterns, skills gaps, and job creation/loss
- **Infrastructure**: The Horizon Fund helps finance data center water and energy infrastructure improvements
- **Security**: Advanced AI Security Readiness Act (bipartisan, with Sen. Todd Young) — security standards for AI systems
- **Military AI**: Guidance on AI use in NDAA, particularly regarding Pentagon-AI company relationships
- **Public trust**: Bipartisan legislation for public awareness and AI literacy campaigns

**Regulatory philosophy**: Government-facilitated redistribution and proactive workforce adjustment. Kelly's approach treats AI primarily as an economic disruption problem requiring institutional investment, not just a safety or innovation problem. His emphasis on industry-funded transition mechanisms (rather than taxpayer-funded programs) and labor data infrastructure distinguishes him from both the White House and progressive approaches.

---

### 5. Sen. Bernie Sanders — AI Policy Proposals (2025–2026)

Sanders represents the most interventionist and structurally critical position in the current debate, treating AI governance as inseparable from questions of corporate power and wealth inequality.

**Key proposals:**

- **Data center moratorium**: National moratorium on new data center construction to "give democracy a chance to catch up." Cites impacts on land, water, electricity costs, and job displacement
- **Robot tax**: Tax on corporations that replace workers with machines, with revenue directed to displaced workers
- **Break up AI companies**: Calls for breaking up OpenAI and other major AI companies; frames concentration of AI power in a few corporations as fundamentally antidemocratic
- **Worker ownership**: Increased employee ownership of AI companies; reduced workweek
- **Job displacement warning**: Released report warning AI could eliminate nearly 100 million U.S. jobs in a decade

**Regulatory philosophy**: Structural intervention and democratic control. Sanders views AI not as a technology policy problem but as a power and inequality problem. His proposals — moratoriums, breakups, robot taxes — are tools of political economy, not technical governance. He is the only major voice calling for a pause in AI infrastructure buildout, making his position the most precautionary in the landscape.

---

### 6. Rep. Ro Khanna — Seven Principles for Democratic AI (February 2026)

Khanna, presented at Stanford alongside Sanders, articulates a middle path between Silicon Valley optimism and progressive structural critique. As a representative of a Silicon Valley district, his position carries particular weight.

**Seven principles:**

1. **Keep humans in the loop**: Protections against mass displacement; human drivers must remain in trucks even as autonomous systems improve; AI should augment rather than replace
2. **Worker benefits from productivity**: Workers must share in AI-driven productivity gains; tax credits for companies that give employees a stake in AI-generated profits
3. **Worker board representation**: Workers should be represented on AI company boards; rank-and-file consulted on AI deployment in public and private sectors
4. **Community benefits**: Communities hosting data centers must benefit from AI infrastructure; companies cannot extract without reinvesting locally
5. **Guardrails on deployment**: Worker input required before AI tools are deployed in workplaces; legislation giving workers a voice in adoption
6. **Anti-excessive automation**: Tax reforms to discourage excessive automation; making it easier to hire humans over AI agents
7. **Mandatory regulation with third-party verification**: Enforceable guardrails with mandatory third-party verification of advanced AI models

**Regulatory philosophy**: Worker-centered with market-compatible mechanisms. Khanna explicitly rejects Luddism ("not for a complete ban") while insisting on structural mechanisms (board representation, tax incentives, mandatory verification) that embed worker and community interests into AI governance. His approach is more market-compatible than Sanders' but more structurally interventionist than Kelly's.

### 7. California: SB 1047 (2024, vetoed) → SB 53 (2025, signed)

California's evolution from SB 1047 to SB 53 illustrates the real-time negotiation between ambition and political feasibility in AI governance.

**SB 1047 (2024, vetoed by Gov. Newsom):**
- Required frontier AI developers to implement safety testing before deployment
- Included a "kill switch" requirement for shutting down dangerous models
- Created rigid pre-deployment testing regime
- Imposed liability for catastrophic harms

**SB 53 — Transparency in Frontier AI Act (2025, signed):**
- Targets "large frontier developers" — companies with >$500M annual revenue developing models above certain computational thresholds
- Requires transparency reports on safety testing and precautions
- Critical safety incident reporting: 15 days standard, 24 hours if imminent harm
- No kill switch requirement; no rigid testing regime
- Transparency-first rather than compliance-first approach
- Beginning to incorporate CAISI-aligned standards

**Other notable California AI bills (2025):**
- AB 853: Delays AI Transparency Act implementation; adds platform disclosure requirements
- SB 243: Companion chatbot regulation — notice requirements and anti-suicide-ideation protocols
- AB 325: Algorithmic pricing under antitrust law

**Regulatory philosophy**: Iterative, transparency-driven, with voluntary alignment toward federal standards. California's shift from SB 1047's mandate-heavy approach to SB 53's transparency-first model reflects Newsom's preference for disclosure over prohibition and the political difficulty of imposing pre-deployment requirements on frontier developers headquartered in the state.

---

### 8. New York RAISE Act (2025, signed)

New York's Responsible AI Safety and Education Act establishes reporting and safety governance for frontier AI developers, signed by Governor Hochul.

**Key provisions:**

- Covers companies with >$500M in revenue developing frontier models
- Developers must create, maintain, and publicly disclose comprehensive safety and security protocols including: administrative, technical, and physical cybersecurity protections; mitigation strategies for "critical harms"
- Incident reporting to the State within 72 hours of determining an incident occurred
- Civil penalties: $1M for initial violations, escalating to $3M for repeat offenses
- Effective March 19, 2026

**Other notable New York AI bills:**
- AI Act (S1169/A8884): Regulates AI to prevent algorithmic discrimination; requires independent audits of high-risk AI; AG enforcement plus private right of action
- AI Bill of Rights (A3265): Establishes rights including: safe and effective systems, protection from algorithmic discrimination, abusive data practice protections, right to know when automated systems are used, right to opt out, right to human alternative

**Regulatory philosophy**: Compliance-oriented with meaningful penalties. New York's approach is notably more enforcement-heavy than California's SB 53, with specific dollar penalties and shorter reporting windows. The RAISE Act's alignment with CAISI standards signals willingness to harmonize with federal frameworks, consistent with OpenAI's advocated sequencing.

## Part II: Comparative Analysis

### Dimension 1: Governance Structure

The proposals diverge sharply on who should regulate AI and through what mechanisms.

**Existing agencies vs. new institutions**: The White House framework and Blackburn bill rely primarily on existing agencies (FTC, NIST, DOL). Kelly proposes a new funding mechanism (AI Horizon Fund) but not a new regulatory body. Sanders implicitly calls for new authority through moratorium power. OpenAI advocates for CAISI as a quasi-new federal institution with pre-deployment testing authority.

**Voluntary vs. mandatory**: The White House leans voluntary with targeted mandates. Blackburn, despite deregulatory framing, creates substantial mandatory obligations. OpenAI endorses mandatory federal testing. State laws (CA, NY) create mandatory disclosure and reporting. Sanders and Khanna both call for mandatory mechanisms.

**Enforcement pathways**: The Blackburn bill is the most enforcement-dense, creating overlapping FTC, state AG, and private right of action pathways. New York's RAISE Act has specific monetary penalties. California's SB 53 relies more on transparency pressure than punitive enforcement.

### Dimension 2: Scope and Application

**What gets regulated**: All proposals focus on "frontier" or advanced AI systems, but define the threshold differently. California and New York both use $500M revenue thresholds. The Blackburn bill uses FTC-established thresholds ($500M revenue or 1M+ individuals' data processed). Sanders' moratorium applies to infrastructure rather than models.

**Pre-deployment vs. post-deployment**: This is one of the sharpest divides. OpenAI advocates pre-deployment testing (prevention model). California SB 1047 attempted this but was vetoed; SB 53 retreated to post-deployment transparency. The Blackburn bill creates an evaluation program but the mandatory testing scope is ambiguous. Sanders' moratorium is the most aggressive pre-deployment intervention — stopping the infrastructure itself. The White House framework leans post-deployment (enforcement of existing laws).

**Sectoral vs. horizontal**: Most proposals are horizontal (applying across sectors). Kelly's NDAA provisions are a notable exception, targeting military AI specifically. The Blackburn bill includes sector-specific provisions for children's platforms alongside horizontal obligations.

### Dimension 3: Political Positioning

**Innovation-friendly vs. precautionary**: The White House and OpenAI positions are explicitly innovation-friendly, though both support some safety requirements. Sanders is the most precautionary. Khanna occupies a middle ground, and Kelly tilts toward managed innovation with redistribution.

**Industry-backed vs. civil-society-backed**: OpenAI's position is the most explicit industry voice. The White House framework aligns closely with industry preferences on preemption and light regulation. Sanders and Khanna represent civil-society/labor perspectives. Kelly bridges these, proposing industry-funded mechanisms.

**Federal vs. state**: The White House and Blackburn are the most aggressive on preemption. OpenAI supports preemption but with a stronger federal framework as precondition. California and New York are asserting state authority while signaling willingness to defer to adequate federal standards. Sanders and Khanna have not made preemption a central focus.

## Part IV: Key Themes and Emerging Consensus

Despite sharp disagreements, several areas of convergence are identifiable across the political spectrum.

### Areas of Broad Agreement

**1. Frontier models require differentiated treatment.** Every proposal — from the White House to Sanders — treats the most powerful AI systems as requiring some form of distinct governance. The $500M revenue threshold used by California and New York is emerging as a de facto standard, and the Blackburn bill uses a similar approach.

**2. Child safety is a priority.** The White House, Blackburn, California (SB 243), and OpenAI all prioritize child safety provisions. This is the area of greatest bipartisan alignment and the most likely candidate for near-term legislation.

**3. Transparency and disclosure have value.** Even the lightest-touch proposals (White House) endorse some form of transparency. SB 53, the RAISE Act, and the Blackburn bill all include reporting and disclosure requirements. The debate is over whether transparency alone is sufficient or whether it must be paired with pre-deployment testing or liability.

**4. Workers will be affected and something should be done.** The Blackburn bill's AI-related job effects reporting, Kelly's Horizon Fund, Sanders' robot tax, and Khanna's board representation proposals all acknowledge AI-driven workforce disruption. The disagreement is over mechanism: reporting vs. redistribution vs. structural intervention.

**5. Copyright and digital replicas need addressing.** The White House, Blackburn (NO FAKES Act, content provenance), and the broader debate all converge on the need for some federal framework on AI-generated replicas and content provenance. Copyright training data remains unresolved, with the White House explicitly deferring to courts.

### Areas of Sharp Disagreement

**1. Preemption scope.** The deepest divide. The White House and Blackburn want broad preemption of state AI development regulation. OpenAI wants preemption contingent on a meaningful federal framework. States are asserting authority while signaling willingness to defer. Sanders, Khanna, and Kelly have not prioritized preemption.

**2. Pre-deployment vs. post-deployment intervention.** OpenAI's prevention-first model (federal testing before deployment) is endorsed by no other major proposal in its strong form. The vetoed SB 1047 was the closest state-level analog. Most proposals rely on post-deployment accountability.

**3. Economic structure of AI.** Sanders' moratorium and breakup proposals have no analogs elsewhere. Kelly's industry-funded Horizon Fund is a moderate redistribution mechanism. The White House and Blackburn contain no redistribution provisions.

**4. The role of liability.** The Blackburn bill creates extensive liability frameworks (developer, deployer, federal cause of action). The White House prefers existing law. New York's proposed AI Act includes a private right of action. The appropriate role of litigation in AI governance remains unresolved.

## Part V: What's Missing

Several significant governance questions receive little or no attention across the current proposals:

**Open-source AI governance.** Most proposals focus on large commercial developers. The treatment of open-source frontier models — which create different risk profiles and different enforcement challenges — is largely unaddressed.

**International coordination mechanisms.** OpenAI and the Blackburn bill reference international cooperation, but no proposal establishes concrete mechanisms for aligning U.S. AI governance with EU, UK, or multilateral approaches.

**Algorithmic accountability in government use.** While the Blackburn bill's Title XVI addresses agency use of AI and New York's AI Bill of Rights proposes opt-out rights, the specific governance of AI in law enforcement, benefits administration, and other high-stakes government contexts remains underdeveloped.

**Environmental impact.** Sanders' moratorium is the only proposal that directly addresses the environmental footprint of AI infrastructure. The White House framework addresses energy through the lens of ratepayer protection but not environmental sustainability.

**Concentration and competition.** Beyond Sanders' breakup calls, no proposal addresses the competitive dynamics of AI development — the concentration of compute, data, and talent among a small number of firms and its implications for innovation and governance.

## Methodology and Sources

This analysis is based on primary source documents where available (White House legislative recommendations, Blackburn bill text, OpenAI Substack newsletter) supplemented by news coverage and legislative summaries for proposals where full legislative text was not available at time of writing. For the Kelly, Sanders, and Khanna proposals, we relied on published roadmaps, op-eds, press releases, and reporting from Axios, The Hill, NBC News, FedScoop, and Stanford Daily. California and New York bill provisions are drawn from legislative text summaries by IAPP, Fisher Phillips, Future of Privacy Forum, Davis Wright Tremaine, and Alston & Bird.

Analytical placement on 2×2 charts involves interpretive judgment. Reasonable analysts may disagree about the precise positioning of proposals, particularly where a proposal's stated philosophy diverges from its operative provisions (as with the Blackburn bill's deregulatory framing versus its regulatory density).
